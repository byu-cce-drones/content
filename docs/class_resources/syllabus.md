## Description

This 1-credit freshman course introduces students to drone applications in civil and environmental engineering. Students will learn foundational drone safety, flight procedures, basic surveying concepts, GIS workflows, mission planning, photogrammetry processing, and FAA Part 107 preparation. The course combines short lectures, hands-on labs, field activities, data-processing exercises, and a group final project.

Students will begin with small indoor drones to build confidence with controls and flight safety before progressing to larger mission-capable drones. The course emphasizes professional field habits, including checklists, careful preparation, safety awareness, teamwork, and responsible data collection.

By the end of the semester, students will understand how drones can be used for engineering documentation, mapping, inspection, measurement, and project communication.

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Explain common civil engineering uses for drones, including surveying, mapping, inspection, construction documentation, and environmental monitoring.
2. Describe basic FAA Part 107 rules and identify when a remote pilot certificate is required.
3. Demonstrate safe drone operation practices, including pre-flight preparation, situational awareness, and checklist use.
4. Operate mini drones in a controlled indoor environment using basic flight maneuvers.
5. Prepare and use equipment and procedure checklists for drone fieldwork.
6. Explain the difference between accuracy and precision in measurement tasks.
7. Use basic GIS and mapping tools, including Google Earth and QGIS, to support measurement and mapping workflows.
8. Plan a drone mapping mission using appropriate flight parameters, overlap, altitude, coverage, and site constraints.
9. Describe the basic principles of photogrammetry and drone data processing.
10. Compare different drone sensor types, including RGB cameras, LiDAR, thermal, multispectral, and radar-based systems.
11. Work in a team to plan, fly, process, and communicate results from a drone-based project.
12. Present drone project results clearly through written, visual, and oral communication.

### Part 107 Practice Test Policy

The Part 107 practice test is intended to help students prepare for the real FAA Remote Pilot Certificate exam. The course practice test will include 60 questions and a 2-hour time limit. The passing threshold is 70%, or 42 out of 60 questions.

The practice test will use proctored-style settings, including one question per page and access to the FAA supplement as a PDF resource.

Students will receive two automatic attempts during the assigned test window. After the first attempt, students may review missed questions to guide further study.

A third attempt requires a brief consultation with a TA. Before taking a third attempt, the student must complete corrections for the lowest score from the first two attempts.

Students who pass may complete corrections to improve their assignment score, subject to instructor guidelines. If a student takes a third attempt and still does not earn 70%, corrections on the third attempt may raise the score up to, but not above, 70%.

The purpose of this policy is to encourage preparation, allow targeted learning, and help students develop confidence before taking the real FAA exam.

## Classroom Procedures

### Before Class

Students should complete assigned readings and pre-class quizzes before coming to class. Reading quizzes are usually designed as completion and preparation checks. All quizzes are open book and will allow only one attempt to encourage careful preparation before class.

### During Lecture

Most lectures will be discussion-based and centered around the assigned readings. Students are expected to come prepared to engage with the material, ask questions, and contribute to class conversations. Lectures will often focus on applying concepts, discussing real-world examples, clarifying difficult topics, and connecting readings to lab activities and engineering practice.

Students should bring a laptop or device when needed for mapping, GIS, mission planning, or test-prep activities. Students are expected to participate respectfully, take notes, ask questions, and stay engaged.

### During Labs

Students must follow all lab safety instructions. Some labs will require students to work in assigned groups or lab sections. Flight labs may use sign-up windows to manage equipment and instructor supervision.

Students may only operate any drone used in this course, including mini drones, after earning their TRUST certificate. Before each flight, there will be a safety brief. Students must follow all safety instructions and TA or instructor guidance during the lab.

Except for the mini drones used in introductory indoor labs, all course drones may only be operated when a certified TA or instructor holding a current FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate is present and supervising the flight operation. Only one larger drone may be airborne per certified TA or instructor at a time. This policy applies even if a student already holds their own Part 107 certificate. Students may not independently operate course drones outside of supervised course activities.

### Drone and Equipment Expectations

Students are expected to handle all course equipment carefully. This includes drones, batteries, controllers, chargers, SD cards, laptops, and other field equipment.

Students should follow all instructor and TA instructions for:

* Battery handling.
* Drone setup.
* Controller setup.
* Calibration.
* Site safety checks.
* Takeoff and landing.
* Data storage.
* Equipment return.

### Checklist Culture

A major theme of the course is that safe and reliable pilots operate by checklist. Students will practice creating, using, and improving checklists throughout the semester.

Checklists should include both:

1. **Equipment lists** — items required for the mission, such as charged batteries, SD cards, controllers, chargers, laptops, and field supplies.
2. **Procedure lists** — step-by-step actions before, during, and after flight.

Students will also have access to a more detailed course flight checklist that will be used throughout the semester during labs and field activities. (Add hyperlink to checklist when available.)

## Teaching Philosophy

This course is built around hands-on learning, curiosity, and professional habits. Drones are exciting tools, but they are also aircraft, data-collection systems, and engineering instruments. Students will be expected to approach drone work with both enthusiasm and responsibility.

The course uses a “think first, then refine” approach. For example, students may first create a checklist before being shown a professional example. This helps students identify what they already understand, discover what they missed, and appreciate why formal procedures matter.

Labs are designed to help students learn by doing. Mistakes, questions, and revisions are part of the learning process, especially when students are working with new tools, software, and field procedures. At the same time, safety-related instructions must be followed carefully and immediately.

The goal of the course is not only to teach students how drones work, but also to help them think like responsible engineers: prepared, observant, collaborative, precise, and safety-minded.
